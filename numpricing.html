What is financial derivative?

A derivative is a contract between two or more parties whose value is based on an agreed-upon underlying financial asset (like a security) or set of assets (like an index). Common underlying instruments include bonds, commodities, currencies, interest rates, market indexes and stocks.

Derivatives can be used for several purposes, including insuring against price movements (hedging), increasing exposure to price movements for speculation or getting access to otherwise hard-to-trade assets or markets. Generally, derivatives are used to scatter the risks which is contained in the transactions and operating businesses.

They are now becoming increasingly vital for the market and the society as a part of the financial market, a sensible way to combine computing and derivatives will be of potentiality.

How are the prices of the financial derivatives determined?

Typical financial derivatives such as futures and options are used to describe the goods and services which have not been generated and produced yet, so it can possibly be simplified to be guaranteed transactions made in the future.

Futures contracts (futures) are financial contracts to purchase or sell a certain kind of good or service in the future. Therefore, the value of which will be based on the commodity's market price. For example, assume that the chair futures contract that represents 1,000 sets of chairs. If a set of chairs are trading at a price of 3 units of a currency, the value of the contract is 3,000 units of a currency. Futures contracts are standardized to include a certain amount and quality of the underlying commodity, so they can be traded on a centralized exchange. The futures price moves in relation to the spot price for the commodity based on supply and demand for that commodity.

Options on stocks and exchange-traded funds are also common derivative contracts. Options give the buyer the right, compared to the obligation that is contained in the future contracts. The best-known pricing model for options is the Black-Scholes method. (Black-Scholes is a pricing model used to determine the fair price or theoretical value for a call or a put option based on six variables such as volatility, type of option, underlying stock price, time, strike price, and risk-free rate), which is formed by the basic formula of Call Option Premium C = SN(d1) - Xe- rt N(d2), Put Option Premium P = Xe-rT N (-d2) - S0 N (-d1), in which call stands for the right to purchase whereas put means the right to sell.

Links to hedging/hedge fund.

Although being slightly different to typical cases, hedging is often brought together with financial derivatives. It just simply indicates a way to offset the potential risks by carrying out the opposite action; for instance, to reduce the impacts brought by the decreasing exchange rates, MNCs (multi-national corporations) usually purchase the counter currency (the currency of whom they are trading with) in advance. To accurately estimate the specific action which is needed to be carried out, a chain of calculation and predictions are essential. For the same reason with pricing the typical financial derivatives, simplifications are necessary to make the process workable.
